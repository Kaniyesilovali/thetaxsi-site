<p>Nicosia is the world's last divided capital: an old city inside a ring of Venetian walls, cut in two by the Green Line that runs through its middle. For a visitor that means something unusual — two city centres a few hundred metres apart, both walkable in an afternoon. Nicosia is also the closest city to Ercan Airport: <strong>20 minutes, 18 km, a fixed £45</strong>, which makes it the quickest place on the island to land and settle into. Here's how to reach it from all three airports, what to see inside the walls, how the Ledra Street crossing works, and how to get around a city that isn't built for hailing taxis.</p>

<h2>Inside the walls: things to do in Nicosia</h2>
<p>Almost everything worth seeing sits inside the Venetian walls, within walking distance of everything else. Half a day covers the main stops; a full day covers both sides of the city.</p>
<ul>
  <li><strong>Büyük Han</strong> — built in 1572, just after the Ottoman conquest, and the largest caravanserai on the island. Two storeys of arcades around a courtyard with a small mosque and fountain in the middle. It served for a while as a prison under British rule; today it's craft workshops and shaded tables where you drink your coffee. The most pleasant pause in the city.</li>
  <li><strong>Selimiye Mosque</strong> — begun in the 13th century as the Gothic Cathedral of Saint Sophia, where the Lusignan kings were crowned. It was converted to a mosque in 1570 and two minarets were added. The result is unmistakably Cypriot: a French Gothic west front, pointed arches, minarets. Dress modestly and remove your shoes at the door.</li>
  <li><strong>Bedesten</strong> — right beside Selimiye, a medieval church later used as the Ottoman cloth market. Restored, and now a venue for cultural events.</li>
  <li><strong>Bandabuliya</strong> — the municipal covered market, also next to Selimiye: fruit and vegetables, hellim cheese and olives, spice stalls and small eateries. Mornings are the liveliest.</li>
  <li><strong>Arasta street</strong> — the bazaar street running from Selimiye down towards the Ledra crossing: fabric shops, tailors, souvenirs, coffee. This is where you see the everyday city.</li>
  <li><strong>The Venetian walls</strong> — a circular rampart built between 1567 and 1570 with eleven heart-shaped bastions. Kyrenia Gate to the north and Famagusta Gate to the east still stand; you can walk the moat, and several bastions are now parks or squares.</li>
</ul>
<p>Midday heat inside the walls is heavy in summer, so keep sightseeing to the early morning or late afternoon. Mosque and museum hours shift around prayer times and weekends — check at the door.</p>
<h2>Crossing at Ledra Street (Lokmacı)</h2>
<p>At the end of Arasta street is the <strong>Ledra Street / Lokmacı pedestrian crossing</strong>, which joins the two halves of the old city. You cross on foot and it takes minutes: a checkpoint on each side, passports shown, no fee to cross. On the far side begins <strong>Ledra Street</strong>, the south's main shopping street — shops, cafés, a steady crowd of pedestrians. The Cyprus Museum is also on the southern side.</p>
<p>A typical day looks like this: Selimiye, the Bedesten and Büyük Han in the morning, lunch at Bandabuliya, then through the crossing in the afternoon for Ledra Street and the south, back through the same gate before evening. The two sides use different currencies; cards work in most places, but a little cash in each makes life simpler.</p>
